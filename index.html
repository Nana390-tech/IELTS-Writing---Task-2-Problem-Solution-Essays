What is a problem-solution essay?

A problem-solution essay asks you to explain a problem and suggest practical ways to fix it. Your answer should be clear, organised, and fully relevant to the question.

The problem

You describe one or two real difficulties — and explain why they matter.

The solution

You give realistic fixes that directly match the problems you raised.

Band 6 goal

Clear and simple beats confusing and complicated. Explain ideas — don't just list them.

Your 4-paragraph map

1

Introduction

Paraphrase the topic. Say the essay will discuss problems and solutions. 2 sentences only.

2

Problems

Explain 1–2 problems. For each: add a reason, example, or result.

3

Solutions

Give 1–2 realistic solutions that match the problems.

4

Conclusion

Summarise the main problem + main solution. No new ideas.

Structure & the memory rule

Use 4 clear paragraphs. Do not make the structure complicated. Inside each body paragraph, follow the same simple chain.

A clear 4-paragraph shape gives you marks for Coherence & Cohesion and keeps your answer fully relevant to the Task Response. Examiners reward organisation they can follow easily.

Band 6 model answer annotated

Key: problem language · solution language · linkers

In many schools, students are using mobile phones for many hours every day. Although phones can be useful for learning, too much use can create several problems. This essay will discuss the main problems and suggest some practical solutions.

▲ Introduction — paraphrases the topic, then signposts problems + solutions. Two sentences of content, no opinion needed.

One major problem is that mobile phones can reduce students' concentration. Many students check messages, watch short videos, or use social media when they should be studying. For example, a student may try to do homework but stop many times to look at a phone notification. As a result, the student may take longer to finish the work and may not understand the lesson well. Another problem is that too much phone use can affect sleep, especially when students use their phones late at night.

▲ Problems — follows Problem → Explain → Example → Result, then adds a second problem. Notice the linkers do the organising.

A practical solution is for schools to set clear rules about phone use. For instance, students could keep their phones in their bags during lessons and only use them when the teacher allows it for learning. Parents also need to help by setting limits at home, such as no phones during homework time or before sleeping. This would help students focus better and develop healthier habits.

▲ Solutions — each solution matches a problem above (focus ↔ rules, sleep ↔ home limits). That matching is what earns Task Response marks.

In conclusion, too much mobile phone use can cause poor concentration and unhealthy routines. However, this problem can be reduced if schools and parents work together and set clear, realistic rules.

▲ Conclusion — restates the main problem + main solution. No new information.
Why this is a good Band 6 answer: It answers both parts of the question, uses clear paragraphs, explains the ideas, and includes simple examples. The language is clear and controlled.
